Decision Report

New City Job Offer vs. Staying in Current Role

July 15, 2026
01 — The Real Decision

You're choosing between long-term career trajectory and immediate personal stability. The real trade-off isn't the job — it's whether you trust yourself to build a new life in a new city without your existing support system. It's emotionally hard because both things you want (growth and comfort) are genuinely legitimate, and taking one risks losing the other.

02 — The Case for Each Option

Take the new job

Option A
Strongest case: Aligns with your long-term goals, offers better pay and growth. Your gut has already voted for it — that's not nothing. Acting now opens doors that waiting may close.
Hidden upside: New cities force you to build an intentional social circle — friends you choose, not just inherited. This can deepen self-knowledge.
Biggest weakness: Isolation risk is real. Without a plan to build community, loneliness can erode the career gains.
Best if you value: growth, ambition, autonomy

Stay in current role

Option B
Strongest case: You have stability, relationships, and a functioning life. Comfort isn't weakness — it's a foundation that supports good work and mental health.
Hidden upside: Staying creates space to negotiate better terms in your current role or explore other opportunities without the pressure of a deadline.
Biggest weakness: You've already said your gut says "go." Staying against your gut often breeds resentment and quiet regret over time.
Best if you value: security, relationships, roots
03 — Assumption Buster

3 Assumptions You May Be Making

Isolation is inevitable if you move — but loneliness is a function of effort and mindset, not location. People build deep friendships in new cities every day.
Your current comfort will still be there in 2–3 years — but roles change, companies change, and staying doesn't guarantee stability either.
The new job must be perfect to justify the risk — but "better" doesn't have to mean "perfect." Even partial growth is net positive.

2 Biases at Play

Loss aversionStatus quo bias

You're weighting what you might lose (comfort, relationships) more heavily than what you might gain. And familiarity feels safer than it statistically is.

1 Thing You're Definitely Ignoring

The cost of not going. You've framed this as "what if the move fails?" but haven't fully reckoned with what it means to your future self if you turn this down and watch the window close.

05 — Premortem

If New Job Fails in 12 Months

Why it failed:
Underestimated the social isolation — no plan to build community
Role didn't match what was promised at interview
Compared new life to old comfort constantly, never gave it a fair chance
Early warning sign: Still feeling like an outsider at 3 months with no local friendships forming
Prevention: Commit to 3 social activities/month from Day 1 — non-negotiable

If Staying Fails in 12 Months

Why it failed:
Gut regret compounds — you watch others advance while you stay static
New opportunities stop arriving because the market reads you as risk-averse
Resentment creeps into work performance and relationships
Early warning sign: Googling the new city and job role you turned down more than once a week
Prevention: Negotiate a raise or promotion within 60 days of declining
06 — 7-Day Test Plan
Day 1Research cost of living, neighbourhood options, and commute in the new city. Build a realistic budget comparison.
Day 2Look up 3 people you know (or friends-of-friends) in that city who you could lean on early.
Day 3Spend one evening living as if you've already accepted — imagine your Saturday in the new city. Write down what you feel.
Day 4Do the opposite: imagine you declined and stayed. Write what you feel 6 months from now.
Day 5Have an honest conversation with one family member or close friend about what your moving would really mean to them — not your assumption.
Day 6Call or message one person who has made a similar move. Ask what they wish they'd known.
Day 7Decision day: If the budget is workable, you have at least one person in the city, and the Day 3 feeling beat the Day 4 feeling — say yes.
07 — The Verdict
Take the new job.

Your gut already decided — it's been overridden by fear, not by logic. The decision is reversible, the upside is career-defining, and the isolation risk is manageable with deliberate effort. What could flip it: if the 7-day test reveals the financial gap is untenable, or the role expectations are significantly misaligned from what was pitched.

Hard truth: The comfort you're protecting isn't going anywhere — but this opportunity is.
