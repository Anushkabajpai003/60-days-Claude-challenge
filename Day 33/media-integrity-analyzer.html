Chapter One

Headline Detective

Headlines aren't written to inform you — they're written to make you click. Publishers earn money per view, so headlines are often engineered to imply more than the article actually delivers: a vague scare, an absolute claim, a number stripped of context.
Why it matters — the gap between a headline's promise and an article's substance is called headline-body mismatch. It's one of the most common ways readers walk away misinformed, even when the article itself is technically accurate.

Chapter Two

Emotion Detector

Some content isn't trying to inform you at all — it's trying to make you feel something fast: outrage, fear, envy, urgency. Strong emotion short-circuits careful thinking, which is exactly why it spreads faster than calm, accurate posts.
Why it matters — platforms reward whatever keeps you scrolling and reacting. Learning to notice which words are pulling your emotional strings is the fastest way to slow down before you share, buy, or believe something.

Three Habits Worth Keeping

  • 01Read past the headline before reacting — the whole claim usually lives in paragraph three, not the title.
  • 02Notice your emotion first, then ask what word triggered it — strong feeling is often the point, not a side effect.
  • 03Ask "who benefits if I believe this instantly?" before sharing anything that feels urgent.
